Historical Roots

From protest songs to revolutionary murals — a journey through time.

Art Has Always Been Political

Long before galleries and concert halls, art was a tool of survival and resistance. Ancient civilisations used visual symbols, chants, and storytelling to record their struggles, celebrate victories, and pass down wisdom. The idea that art exists in a vacuum, separate from politics and society, is a modern myth.

From the very first cave paintings to the protest posters of the 20th century, creative expression has always reflected — and challenged — the world around it.

A Timeline of Art & Resistance

Ancient World

Cave Paintings & Oral Traditions

The earliest known art — cave paintings in Lascaux and Altamira — served as communal storytelling. Oral traditions in African, Indigenous, and Asian cultures used song and poetry to preserve history and resist cultural erasure.

Renaissance — 15th–16th Century

Art as Power & Subversion

While Renaissance masters like Michelangelo and Leonardo were commissioned by the Church and ruling elites, they also smuggled hidden messages and humanist ideals into their work — subtly challenging the authority that funded them.

18th–19th Century

Romantic Revolt & Abolitionist Art

The Romantic movement rejected industrialisation and celebrated emotion and nature. Meanwhile, literature, poetry, and visual art became essential weapons in the fight to abolish slavery — from Harriet Beecher Stowe's Uncle Tom's Cabin to powerful abolitionist illustrations.

Early 20th Century

Dada, Futurism & the Anti-War Movement

World War I shattered the myth of progress. Artists responded with Dadaism — an anarchic rejection of logic and bourgeois values. Picasso's Guernica (1937) became the defining anti-war artwork after the bombing of a Basque town during the Spanish Civil War.

1930s–1950s

Mexican Muralism & Jazz

Diego Rivera, David Alfaro Siqueiros, and José Clemente Orozco used massive public murals to tell the story of workers, indigenous peoples, and revolution. At the same time, jazz — born from African American communities — broke racial barriers and became a symbol of freedom and cultural pride.

1960s–1970s

Civil Rights, Folk & Counterculture

Bob Dylan, Nina Simone, and Joan Baez turned folk and soul music into anthems of the Civil Rights Movement. Poster art, psychedelic design, and underground comics fuelled the anti-Vietnam War movement and the broader counterculture revolution.

1980s–1990s

Hip-Hop, Punk & Street Art

Hip-hop emerged from the Bronx as a voice for marginalised communities. Punk rock challenged societal norms with raw energy. Graffiti artists like Jean-Michel Basquiat and Keith Haring brought activism to the streets, tackling racism, AIDS, and inequality.

Recurring Themes Across History

Art as Memory

From oral traditions to murals, art preserves the stories that official histories often erase or ignore.

Art as Threat

Authoritarian regimes have always censored, banned, or destroyed art — proving its power to destabilise control.

Art as Call to Action

Protest posters, folk songs, and street art don't just reflect movements — they mobilise them.

Art as Universal Language

A mural in Mexico, a jazz riff in Harlem, a punk song in London — creative resistance transcends borders.

Pablo Picasso — Guernica (1937)

On April 26, 1937, Nazi German and Fascist Italian warplanes bombed the Basque town of Guernica during the Spanish Civil War. Picasso, outraged, created this massive oil painting in just over a month.

The monochrome canvas depicts the horrors of war: dismembered bodies, a screaming horse, a bull, and a mother holding her dead child. Its stark, fragmented style refuses to let the viewer look away.

Guernica toured the world, becoming a universal symbol of anti-war protest. It has been invoked in every major conflict since — from Vietnam to Iraq — and remains one of the most powerful political artworks ever created.

Diego Rivera — Mexican Muralism

After the Mexican Revolution (1910–1920), the government commissioned artists to create large-scale public murals that would educate and inspire a largely illiterate population. Diego Rivera became the most prominent of these muralists.

His murals celebrated the history and struggles of Mexico's workers, farmers, and indigenous communities — placing ordinary people at the centre of history rather than kings and generals. His bold, colourful style made complex political ideas accessible to everyone.

Rivera's work proved that art doesn't need to live in a museum to matter — it can transform a public wall into a classroom, a manifesto, and a monument all at once.
