Video Games as Art

How interactive storytelling and game design tackle social issues and spark empathy.

The Newest Art Form

For decades, video games were dismissed as toys — mindless entertainment for children. But in the 21st century, that perception has shattered. Games now tell stories that rival literature, create worlds as breathtaking as any painting, and force players to confront moral dilemmas that linger long after the screen goes dark.

What makes games unique as an art form is interactivity. You don't just watch injustice unfold — you participate in it. You don't just hear about moral choices — you make them. This transforms passive empathy into lived experience, and that is something no other medium can do.

Games That Challenge & Transform

Narrative-Driven Epics

Games like The Last of Us and Red Dead Redemption 2 deliver cinematic stories exploring morality, loss, and survival — making players emotionally invest in characters who face impossible choices.

Empathy Simulators

Papers, Please puts you in the shoes of a border officer in a totalitarian state. This War of Mine makes you a civilian in a besieged city. These games force you to feel what it’s like to live under systems of oppression.

Visual Masterpieces

Journey, Gris, and Ori and the Blind Forest prove that games can be breathtaking visual art — using colour, light, and composition to evoke emotions without a single word of dialogue.

Political Commentary

BioShock deconstructs Ayn Rand’s objectivism. Spec Ops: The Line turns a military shooter into an anti-war statement. These games weaponise the player’s own actions to deliver their message.

Cultural Exploration

Never Alone (Kisima Ingitchuna) shares Iñupiaq folklore. Okami brings Japanese mythology to life. Games can preserve and celebrate cultures that mainstream media ignores.

Mental Health & Identity

Celeste tackles anxiety and depression. Hellblade: Senua’s Sacrifice portrays psychosis with clinical accuracy. These games destigmatise mental illness by making the player feel it from the inside.

Case in Focus: Red Dead Redemption 2

Rockstar Games’ 2018 masterpiece is arguably the most compelling argument that video games are art. Set in 1899, Red Dead Redemption 2 follows Arthur Morgan, an outlaw grappling with loyalty, morality, and the death of the American frontier.

The game’s world is staggering in its detail: dynamic weather systems that paint the sky in watercolour sunsets, forests where every tree sways independently, wildlife that behaves with documentary realism. But the artistry goes deeper — the game explores the displacement of Native American communities, the brutality of industrialisation, and the widening gap between rich and poor during the Gilded Age.

Player choices carry moral weight. Help a stranger or rob them. Show mercy or seek revenge. The game tracks your honour invisibly, and the story’s ending changes based on who you chose to be — a mechanic that forces genuine self-reflection in a way that passive media never can.

Case in Focus: Papers, Please

Created by solo developer Lucas Pope in 2013, Papers, Please is set in the fictional communist state of Arstotzka. You play as a border checkpoint inspector, checking passports and deciding who enters the country. It sounds mundane — and that’s precisely the point.

The game traps you in an impossible system: your family is starving, your pay depends on processing people quickly, and the rules change daily. A woman begs you to let her through to escape her abusive husband — but her papers are wrong. A father’s passport has expired by one day. Do you follow the rules, or follow your conscience?

Papers, Please doesn’t lecture about authoritarianism — it makes you complicit in it. By the end, you understand how ordinary people become instruments of oppression, not through malice, but through the slow erosion of choice. No film or book has ever made that point so viscerally.

Case in Focus: This War of Mine

While most war games cast you as a soldier, This War of Mine (2014) puts you in control of a group of civilians trapped in a besieged city — inspired by the real Siege of Sarajevo (1992–1996).

You scavenge for food, medicine, and materials. You decide whether to steal from an elderly couple, trade with dangerous strangers, or sacrifice one person’s safety for the group’s survival. Characters become depressed, fall ill, and can die — or lose the will to go on.

The game was so impactful that in 2020 the Polish government added it to its official recommended reading list for schools — the first time a video game was given the same cultural status as literature. It proved that games can educate, memorialise, and build empathy at a level that traditional media struggles to reach.

Why Do Video Games Work as Art?

Interactivity Creates Responsibility

When you pull the trigger, make the choice, or look away, the emotional weight is personal. You can’t blame the director — you did it.

Time Creates Depth

A game can take 30–100 hours to complete. That sustained engagement builds emotional bonds with characters and worlds that a two-hour film cannot match.

Perspective Shifts Identity

Playing as a refugee, a soldier, a prisoner, or a dying man forces you to inhabit a life radically different from your own — and that builds empathy at a neurological level.

Games Combine Every Art Form

Music, visual art, writing, architecture, cinematography, and performance — games synthesise all of these into a single, unified experience.

Red Dead Redemption 2 — Art Through Interactivity

Developed by Rockstar Games and released in 2018, Red Dead Redemption 2 took over eight years and an estimated 2,000 people to create. The result is one of the most detailed open worlds ever built — a living, breathing recreation of America at the turn of the 20th century.

The game’s protagonist, Arthur Morgan, is widely regarded as one of the greatest characters in gaming history. His arc — from loyal outlaw to a man questioning everything he believed in — is a meditation on redemption, legacy, and what it means to leave the world better than you found it.

Beyond storytelling, RDR2’s visual design is extraordinary: hand-painted skyboxes, volumetric lighting, and over 200 species of animals behaving with ecological accuracy. It blurs the line between game and painting, proving that interactive media can achieve the same emotional resonance as any traditional art form.
